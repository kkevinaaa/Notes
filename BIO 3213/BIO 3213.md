# Protein Structure

**Primary:** amino acid sequence

**Secondary structure:** alpha helix, beta sheet and loops
- Formed and stabilized by hydrogen bonding, electrostatic and van der Waals interactions.
**Tertiary:** Phi-Psi angle
- Protein packing yields tertiary structure.
**Quaternary:** arrangement of several polypeptide chains
- Involves interaction among tertiary structure elements of separate polymer chains.

### The Torsion or Dihedral Angles

![[dad22c00cc41a014b3ce2a00ea823f90.jpg]]
> 从中心$C_\alpha$往两侧观察，顺时针为正，逆时针为负。向N方向为$\phi$，向C方向为$\psi$。
> $\phi$比较两个肽平面上羟基C的角度差异，$\psi$比较两个肽平面上氨基N的角度差异

**Peptide Bond Planar:** **Peptide bonds cannot rotate** because they are partial double bonds. The nitrogen atom in the peptide bond bears a lone pair of electrons in a p orbital, which overlaps with the π-electron system of the adjacent carbonyl group (C=O), forming a **p-π conjugated system**.

### Steric Clash Restricts Allowed Conformations

==Most backbone conformations are forbidden because they produce steric clashes==. It also introduces the **van der Waals radius** as a way to describe how close non-bonded atoms can approach one another: the side chains of amino acids may have large steric bulk and could create large steric hindrance.

###  (1) Electrostatic Interactions

- **Charge-charge interaction:** long-ranged interaction
	Coulomb’s law:  $U = \frac{q_i q_j}{\epsilon_r r_{ij}}$ 
	 $𝜀_r$ is the dielectric constant of the medium.
- **Charge-dipole interaction**
- **Dipole-dipole interaction**
	Dipole-Dipole interactions occur between molecules (polar) that have permanent dipoles.
	>A dipole forms when two covalently bonded atoms have different **electronegativities**. Even if total molecular charge is zero, positive and negative charge centers may not overlap, creating **partial charges**.
	>Dipole Moment $\mu$: $\mu = qr$ 
		𝑞 is the magnitude of the charges (C), 𝑟 is the separation between charges (m)

> Peptide bond polarity
>  ![[Pasted image 20260321204922.png]]
> The orientation of the dipole moment of a peptide is approximately parallel to the N-H bond and is around 3.7 Debye in magnitude.

- **Van der Waals interactions:** short-ranged interaction
	A randomly produced electronic dipole in one atom polarizes an adjacent atom and induces it to form an opposite dipole.
	$U_{wdW} \approx \frac{A_{ij}}{r_{ij}^{12}} - \frac{B_{ij}}{r_{ij}^{6}}$

### (2) Nonpolar Interactions and the Hydrophobic Effect
Nonpolar solutes tend to aggregate in water
$\Delta G_{np} \approx -0.025\Delta SA$
	$Δ𝐺_{np}$ is the nonpolar interaction free energy (in kcal/mol)
	$ΔSA$ is the surface area of the molecule involved in the interaction

>Noncovalent interactions are transient but essential. The transient nature of noncovalent interactions allows for **protein dynamics and function**.
